1. The 1 / 2-4 / 5+ Rule

The form of a noun changes based on the number preceding it:

  • 1: Singular — 1 godzina, 1 rok
  • 2, 3, 4: Nominative Plural — 3 godziny, 3 lata
  • 5+: Genitive Plural — 5 godzin, 5 lat

⚠️ Exception: The teens (12, 13, 14) always use the Genitive Plural (5+ rule), not the 2-4 rule. So "12 lat" not "12 lata".

2. Gender: 1 & 2

Only the numbers 1 and 2 change to match grammatical gender:

1: jeden (m.), jedna (f.), jedno (n.)

2: dwa (m./n.), dwie (f.)

Example: jeden stół, jedna lampa, jedno okno

3. Telling the Time

Formal (24h): Hours as feminine ordinals, then minutes as cardinals.
13:50 → "trzynasta pięćdziesiąt"

Casual (12h):

  • On the hour: druga (2:00)
  • Half past: wpół do trzeciej (2:30)
  • Minutes past: dziesięć po drugiej (2:10)
  • Minutes to: za dziesięć druga (1:50)
4. Dates

Naming (Nominative) — "What day is it?"
Day = Masculine Ordinal (e.g. piąty), Month = Genitive (e.g. maja).
Dzisiaj jest piąty maja.

Event (Genitive) — "On which date?"
Day ordinal ends in -ego.
Piątego maja mam spotkanie.

Year: Always followed by roku (Genitive of rok).
... dwa tysiące dwudziestego czwartego roku.

Each mode targets a specific area of Polish numbers and grammar.

123 Numbers

Core counting. Integers, fractions, decimals, and negatives.

💬 Context (Sentences)

Numbers in real sentences with correct noun declension. Digit buttons are disabled — you must choose the correctly spelled Polish word.

$$ Prices

Prices spoken naturally as in everyday shops. "Pięć pięćdziesiąt" (5,50 zł).

🕒 Time

Clock reading in Casual and Formal styles. Links to the Polish Clock app in the suite.

📅 Dates

Ordinal numbers and months, Nominative vs Genitive cases. Links to the Polish Date Master for calendar practice.

🚻 Gender

Correct form of 1 and 2 based on noun gender (jeden/jedna/jedno, dwa/dwie).

🥇 Ordinals
